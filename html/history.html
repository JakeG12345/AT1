The Internet - A Brief History

Developing The Internet

In the 1960s, data exchange was possible but unreliable, and a new way of sending data was needed. Leonard Kleinrock published the concept of packet switching in 1961, which involved splitting data into small packets and transmitting them along different routes using routers. This was a significant advancement in networking, and in 1969, the United States Defense Department's ARPANET linked four computers, paving the way for many other institutions to join over the next 20 years.

New Protocols

The development of transmission control protocol and Internet protocol (TCP/IP) in 1974 allowed for the connection of different networks and the birth of the modern internet. The domain name system (DNS) was introduced in the early 1980s to divide internet addresses into logical groups, and protocols such as HTTP and HTML were developed to enable the exchange of images and sounds on the internet. Private providers also began offering the public access to the internet, leading to the continual upgrading of network infrastructure to accommodate the growing number of users and increasing demand for high-speed broadband access.

Mobile Phones And The Internet

In 1996, Nokia launched the Nokia 9000 Communicator; the first mobile phone with internet connectivity. However, accessing the Internet on mobile devices at that time was still difficult. It wasn't until 1999 that Japan launched the world's first mobile Internet service. Then, in 2001, in the United States, the BlackBerry速 smartphone was introduced with most mobile device Internet services using the WAP (Wireless Access Protocol) to efficiently operate on small screens and tiny keypads. It was soon reported by developing countries that the majority of their Internet users accessed the Internet through a mobile phone rather than a desktop or laptop computer. By 2008, there were more mobile phones with Internet access than personal computers. In fact, in many parts of the developing world, there were 10 mobile phone users for every personal computer user.
