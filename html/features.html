The Internet - Features

Connecting

Optical Fiber Cable Diagram

Connecting to the Internet requires a computer, data connection, software for access, an ISP account, and a modem or similar equipment. Traditional telephone connections use modems to convert digital signals to analog signals, while broadband connections are faster and can use optical fiber cable, DSL, ISDN, or wireless technology. ISDN lines offer faster connection rates than dial-up lines and use multiplexing, while DSL divides telephone lines into channels for data and phone calls. ADSL is a common form of DSL that prioritizes download speeds. Modems or routers are the main connection hardware used for ISDN, ADSL, and cable.

World Wide Web

The World Wide Web (WWW) is a network of sites that can be searched and retrieved using the HyperText Transfer Protocol (HTTP). It was designed in 1989 by Tim Berners-Lee and scientists at CERN to make it easier to retrieve research documentation. Today, millions of websites exist for people to find information, share ideas, express opinions, learn online, and be entertained. The web uses hyperlinks to join web pages together and create websites, with a home page usually being the opening page. Newspapers and magazines from all over the world are also available on the web.

What Is An Intranet

An intranet is a private network that uses Internet and World Wide Web protocols to connect networks. It differs from connected computers in a LAN because it follows international standards, such as TCP/IP and HTTP, as communication protocols. While intranet users may have access to the wider Internet, this access is usually controlled. For example, a police department may access databases from the motor registry office to form a private intranet. A school-wide network can also serve as an example of an intranet, with a proxy server used to control access to certain web pages or to speed up access by storing frequently used pages. Intranets can be more complex than the Internet since they may need to connect several local networks, each using different protocols.

Email

Email is a popular internet feature for global communication. It works like a simple word processor, allowing users to edit, save drafts and send messages. The email server controls and stores emails, while the local mail client receives and sends messages. There are two protocols for storage and retrieval of incoming email: post office protocol (POP) and Internet message access protocol (IMAP). IMAP is preferred as it is more flexible and allows multiple devices to access mail. Email can be accessed using stand-alone software or webmail services. An email address consists of a mailbox name, an @ sign and a server address. Messages accumulate in a text file until the receiver logs in to read them. Emails can be sent to thousands of people at once in a bulk mail-out.

Parts of an Email

Email messages have different fields such as To, CC, BCC, Subject, Reply, Reply to all, Forward, and Attachments. Various protocols such as SMTP, MIME, POP, and IMAP work together to send and receive emails. The email stays on the sender's computer until it is sent, then it goes to the service provider's email server, and then to the recipient's ISP email server. Email is instant, automatic, and cheaper than a phone call, but it requires daily computer logins.

Email and Viruses

An email virus is a harmful program contained in an attachment sent with an email, often an executable file or macro. Macro viruses can damage files on the computer's hard drive by copying themselves over them. To avoid email viruses, it is best not to open attachments unless the sender is recognized, and it is clear they meant to send the attachment.

Spam

Electronic mailboxes can receive spam or unwanted email messages sent by mass marketers who obtain email addresses and add them to their mailing lists. Dedicated mail software allows users to create rules to prevent spam from appearing in their inbox, such as trapping mail with specific keywords in the subject line.

Privacy of Email

Email is not entirely private, as it is not sealed like a postcard, and an incorrect address could result in it being delivered to the wrong person, or an ISP may return an automated message stating that the message could not be delivered. Schools and employers often have regulations that permit them to read emails to prevent unacceptable content from being included in emails or attachments.

Email Passwords

Maintaining password security is crucial for protecting your email from unauthorized access. The password should be memorable but not obvious to others, and it's important to exercise caution when using the "remember password" feature in your mail software. While it can be a time-saver if only one person regularly uses the computer, if you don't want others in the household to have access to your email, it's essential to keep your password protected.

Netiquette and Emoticons

Email communication is widely used due to its fast and convenient nature, but it lacks the tone-setting aspect of traditional letter writing. As a solution, email users have developed ground rules, such as avoiding writing in all capitals and using emoticons or texting abbreviations like "LOL" to convey the intended tone and meaning of the message.

Social Networking

Social networking sites like Facebook, Twitter, LinkedIn, Instagram, and Pinterest have become popular ways to connect with people who share common interests or to stay in touch with friends. The 'Like' button on Facebook has become a defining feature of the current generation, and advertisers use it to collect data on users. However, users must be aware that their personal information is being shared with others. Collaborative websites like YouTube, Flickr, and Wikipedia allow users to contribute to a common body of information, resulting in the democratization of the web. Social media is increasingly being used in politics and elections. Newsgroups allow subscribers to communicate with others who share similar interests, with moderated newsgroups being more valuable due to the removal of irrelevant or offensive contributions.

Forums & Message Boards

Message boards are public forums found on many websites where people can post and receive answers. They are accessible from anywhere in the world and are similar to newsgroups, but the information is stored on a single server maintained by the discussion board's owner.

Mailing Lists

A mailing list, also known as a listserv, is a group of email addresses recognized as a single entity. Some lists have restrictions on the number of participants or qualifications required, such as a mailing list for doctors regarding health guidelines. Some mailing lists, like newsgroups, are moderated.

Blogs

Blogs, derived from the words 'web' and 'log', became widely popular by 2004 and there are now millions of individual blogs. They are usually personal websites with text, images, and videos, often in the form of a diary or journal. Visitors can interact with the author through comments. Some bloggers express controversial views and report on current events. The impact of blogging on traditional media is still debated. Twitter is a mini-blog service where users can send and read messages called tweets, limited to 140 characters and delivered to the author's subscribers.

Chat Rooms & Forums

Online chat involves real-time exchange of text messages between connected users, but it can be difficult to keep track of the conversation. Internet relay chat (IRC) or chat rooms are the most popular ways to chat online, but users have no control over who they are messaging. Chat sessions may have an operator to monitor the conversation and remove disruptive users. Forums, unlike chat, are not real-time and allow users to initiate new topics or reply to existing ones. Replies are threaded to make conversations easier to follow.

Newsgroups

Newsgroups are one of the oldest methods of communication on the Internet. They are public discussions on a particular topic that are replicated on many servers around the world. Some newsgroups require a password for participation, such as those set up by schools or universities. The system relies on many computers, known as Usenet or NNTP servers, sharing the task of storing discussions.

Conferencing or Webinars

Conferencing software programs enable virtual meetings between two or more users, also known as webinars. Users can communicate through moving images and sound using webcams, as well as interactive typing. These tutorials are often conducted online, saving organizations money on travel costs. With the advent of broadband internet, video communication has become increasingly popular, with video chat becoming a popular method of online live communication supported by applications such as Skype, AIM, and Facetime.

VoIP

VoIP (voice over Internet protocol) services allow telephone voice calls to be made over the internet, rather than using regular telephone exchanges. Skype is a popular example of a VoIP service, allowing users to download an application to their computer and make free calls to other Skype users. Skype also offers videoconferencing and instant messaging services.

Learning Management Systems

A learning management system (LMS) is an application used for planning a learning sequence that typically has two components: content and communication. The content component might include lesson material, assignments, media, and a record of marks, while the communication component might include announcements, mail, discussions, and chats. Popular examples include Moodle, LAMS, Blackboard, and Edmodo.

Messaging

Instant messaging (IM) is a faster alternative to email as it allows real-time communication and eliminates the need to click "reply" and "send." It's not as secure as email, and each service has its own protocol, so users cannot communicate across platforms. IM can be used for one-on-one conversations, while chat rooms allow multiple users to participate in a group conversation. Facebook, Skype, and ICQ are examples of IM services, and text messaging and multimedia messaging service (MMS) are common examples of IM used with mobile phones.

E-Commerce

E-commerce refers to commercial transactions carried out online, offering advantages such as increased accessibility, immediate updates, and direct dealings between businesses and customers. Disadvantages include impersonal transactions and the risk of theft. Online shopping offers 24/7 accessibility and global choices, while internet banking allows for easy money transfers and bill payments.

Online Gaming

The article discusses the popularity of online games on the web. It mentions that the early online adventure games were known as MUDs, which continued for years and had many followers. With the arrival of fast broadband internet, massively multiplayer online games (MMOGs) involving thousands of players simultaneously have developed from these early MUD games. Popular online games like World of Warcraft, Final Fantasy, Second Life, and The Sims are also discussed. Whole websites are devoted to the most popular games and some people spend a great deal of time inhabiting imagined virtual worlds in which their adventures take place.

Virtual Offices

A virtual office is a type of office that can conduct business from anywhere in the world using communication technology. It can be set up in a home environment, and the business can be continued from anywhere, such as a plane or on holiday.

Virtual Assistants

Virtual assistants are computer programs that can assist users with a variety of tasks through natural language questions and commands. Examples include Apple's Siri, which is built into iOS, and Assistant, a third-party virtual assistant for the Android platform. These assistants can provide information, manage appointments, and even tell jokes.
