The Internet - Issues

Cookies

A cookie is a piece of information stored as a text string on a user's computer, used to keep track of data needed in the future. When a web server sends a cookie, the browser stores it and returns it to the server the next time the page is referenced. Cookies are usually used to store a user ID, which allows websites to accurately determine the number of visitors and which parts of a website are visited by which visitors. However, the risk of privacy invasion is great when linking data-rich profiles and personal information.

Proxy Servers and Firewalls

A proxy server can be used to block access to sites or cache frequently used data, and can act as a firewall to carefully screen all messages. A firewall is a security device used by companies to prevent access by those outside the company, and can inform you of unwanted intrusions. A cable or ADSL connection allows a home computer to have a permanently open connection to the internet, but can also allow greater access to your computer from outside, which can lead to potential security issues such as viruses or other software code.

Virus Protection

Computer viruses can spread through infected applications, disks, attachments, and downloads, and can damage data on your computer by corrupting programs, deleting files, or erasing the entire hard disk. Disinfectant or antivirus programs can be used to remove viruses and repair damaged files. To protect your computer system, virus protection software should be installed to check downloads and incoming emails and should be updated regularly to detect new viruses automatically.

Security - Encryption

Encryption techniques prevent unauthorized access by converting data into an unreadable form without a 'key.' One crucial application of encryption is when transmitting credit card numbers for online purchases. The security of e-commerce is essential for the continued success of online shopping and banking on the internet.

Using The Internet Safely

The Internet is a part of real life and some dangers exist on it, just like in the local environment. Chatting with strangers and sharing personal information can be dangerous, as it is hard to verify the identity of the person on the other end. It is important to keep personal information safe and avoid meeting or trading photos with strangers. Care needs to be taken while using the Internet, and adults should monitor their children's access to it. Additionally, excessive Internet use can lead to addiction and negatively impact relationships.

Digital Certificates

Certificate authorities issue these documents upon request from users, containing their name, serial number, and the authority that issued it. These certificates are utilized to confirm the user's identity during transactions and are commonly stored on their computer.

Truth and The Internet

The Internet is a vast source of information, but not everything found online is true, such as the fictional stories of the Californian VelcroÂ® crop and spaghetti harvests. Therefore, users must exercise caution and not believe everything they read on the Internet, especially when using it for academic or personal research. Unmoderated online encyclopedias like Wikipedia may also contain unreliable information, and users should verify the credentials of the author(s). The Museum of Hoaxes website is an example of this.

Privacy

An individual working for an Internet Service Provider (ISP) would have access to more digital data than what would have been available through telephone taps and mail interception in previous years, giving them ample opportunity to blackmail someone. If one uses the Internet frequently for daily activities, research, or e-commerce, personal information such as financial status, family and friends' names, and other private details may be gathered by third parties. The Privacy Act 1988 (Cwlth) and the NSW Privacy and Personal Information Protection Act 1998 have been established to prevent unauthorized collection or misuse of personal information.

Copyright

The content of a web page, including text, graphics, photos, sound, videos, and clip art, are typically owned by the creator of the page. Just because this content is available on the Internet does not mean it can be used freely. The Australian Copyright Act 1968 (Cwlth) regulates what copying is allowed. Educational institutions and government departments pay a fee to the Copyright Agency Limited (CAL) for making copies of material, which is then distributed to authors and publishers.

Equity

Access to the Internet is not universally available, and not all individuals possess the proficiency to navigate the complex tools required to efficiently obtain information. Financial constraints or a lack of resources may prevent some individuals from obtaining an Internet connection or computer. In rural areas and certain countries, connectivity can be unreliable or absent altogether. In addition, those with disabilities may encounter barriers that impede their ability to access information easily. These disparities exemplify issues of equity, which maintains that all individuals should have equal opportunities to the greatest extent possible. Neglecting these principles can lead to a scenario in which only individuals with financial means or educational opportunities have access to information and, consequently, positions of power and influence.
